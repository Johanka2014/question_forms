Grammar at Work: Questions

Yes/No Questions

As the name suggests, the answer is usually "yes" or "no", but can also express uncertainty. They start with an auxiliary (be, do, have) or modal verb (can, should).

"Is John still working for you?" → "No, he retired last year."

"Has the post arrived yet?" → "I think so."

'Wh-' Questions

These use a question word (who, what, which, where, when, why, how) to ask for specific information. The answer is a piece of information, not "yes" or "no".

"How do you switch on the photocopier?" → "Press that red button on the left."

"Why haven't you gone home yet?" → "I have to finish this report."

Word Order

For the verb 'be', the order is: Question Word (optional) + 'be' + Subject.

"Is Steve a doctor?" / "Why was the train late?"

For all other verbs, an auxiliary verb is needed (be, have, will, do, can, should). The order is: Q-Word (opt) + Auxiliary + Subject + Main Verb.

"Does Susan work for the FBI?" / "When will the project be completed?"

Question Word as Subject

If the question word (who, what, which, whose) is the subject, we do not add an auxiliary like 'do'. The word order is the same as a statement: Subject (Q-Word) + Verb.

"Who left the computer on all night?"

"What made you change your mind?"

Advanced Question Types

Indirect Questions

To be more polite, we introduce a question with a phrase like "Could you tell me...". The question that follows uses statement word order (Subject + Verb).

"Could you tell me when the train leaves?" (not "when does the train leave")

Reply Questions

Short questions used to get more information or to show interest and keep a conversation going.

"I've got an appointment." → "When?"

"I'm flying to Rome." → "Are you? Great!"

Declarative Questions

These have the word order of a statement but the intonation of a question. They often express surprise.

"You're still here? It's nearly eight o'clock!"

Tag Questions

Short questions added to the end of a statement to invite agreement or make a request sound friendlier. A positive statement gets a negative tag, and vice-versa.

"The presentation was excellent, wasn't it?"

"You couldn't lend me a fiver, could you?"
